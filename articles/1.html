In complex analysis, the exponential function $e^{i\theta}$ represents a counterclockwise rotation by an angle of $\theta$ in the complex plane. To determine the rotation matrix for $\operatorname{Re}(e^{i \theta})$ and $\operatorname{Im}(e^{i \theta})$, we can use the Euler's formula for complex exponential: $$e^{i \theta} = \cos(\theta) + i\sin(\theta).$$ The real part of $e^{i \theta}$ is $\operatorname{Re}(e^{i \theta}) = \cos(\theta)$, and the imaginary part is $\operatorname{Im}(e^{i \theta}) = \sin(\theta)$. Now, let's consider the rotation matrix in two dimensions, $\mathbf{R}(\theta)$. This matrix represents a counterclockwise rotation by an angle of $\theta$. It can be written as: $$ \mathbf{R}(\theta) = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -\sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{smallmatrix}\right). $$ Applying this matrix to a vector in the plane will result in a rotation of the vector by the given angle. Both $\operatorname{Re}(e^{i \theta})$ and $\operatorname{Im}(e^{i \theta})$ can be rotated using the same rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}(\theta) = \mathbf{R}(\theta)^{Re} = \mathbf{R}(\theta)^{Im}$, as they are both components of the complex exponential function $e^{i \theta}$. The complex conjugate of $e^{i\theta}$, which is $e^{-i\theta}$, represents a clockwise rotation by an angle of $\theta$ in the complex plane. The associated rotation matrix is the transpose of the rotation matrix for $e^{i\theta}$: $$ \mathbf{R}(\theta)^{-1} = \left( \begin{smallmatrix} \cos \theta & \sin \theta \\ -\sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{smallmatrix}\right). $$ Thus, the difference between the rotation matrices for $e^{i\theta}$ and $e^{-i\theta}$ is a reflection about the main diagonal, which changes the sign of the off-diagonal elements. Let us go through an example of applying a rotation by an angle \(l\theta\) to the decomposed \(e^{i l\alpha}\) into its real and imaginary parts. Given the rotation matrix and the decomposed complex number: $$ \mathbf{R}({l\theta})^{+1} = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l\theta) & -\sin( \theta) \\ \sin(l\theta) & \cos(l\theta) \\ \end{smallmatrix}\right) \quad \quad Y(e^{i l\alpha}) = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l\alpha) \\ \sin(l\alpha) \\ \end{smallmatrix}\right) $$ To apply the rotation matrix to the decomposed complex number, we perform the matrix multiplication: $$ \mathbf{R}({l\theta})^{+1} Y(e^{i l\alpha}) = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l\theta) & -\sin(l\theta) \\ \sin(l\theta) & \cos(l\theta) \end{smallmatrix}\right) \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l\alpha) \\ \sin(l\alpha) \end{smallmatrix}\right) = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l\theta)\cos(2\alpha) - \sin(l\theta)\sin(l\alpha) \\ \sin(l\theta)\cos(2\alpha) + \cos(l\theta)\sin(l\alpha) \\ \end{smallmatrix}\right) $$ The expression can be further simplified by using trigonometric identities for the sum of angles. We can apply the following trigonometric identities: \begin{align*} \cos(\alpha + \beta) &= \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta) \\ \sin(\alpha + \beta) &= \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) + \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta) \end{align*} Using these identities, we can simplify the expression: $$ \mathbf{R}({l \theta})^{+1} Y(e^{i l \alpha}) = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l\theta + l\alpha) \\ \sin(l\theta + l\alpha) \end{smallmatrix}\right) = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos(l(\theta + \alpha)) \\ \sin(l(\theta + \alpha)) \end{smallmatrix}\right) $$ This represents the real and imaginary parts of the rotated complex number $e^{i l \alpha}$ after applying the rotation matrix $\mathbf{R}({l\theta})^{+1}$. Let us revisit the concept of steerable basis functions. In practical applications, we often work with real-valued functions. It is worth noting that the real and imaginary parts of the basis functions correspond to $\cos(l \alpha)$ and $\sin(l \alpha)$, respectively. To simplify our computations, we can stack these real and imaginary parts together. Having stacked the real an imaginary part, the representation matrix turns into its real-valued equivalent. Now, we have blocks of $2 \times 2$ matrices stacked diagonally, each one being an equivalent of a complex representation. For instance, $$ \rho_l(\theta) = e^{-i l \theta} = \left(\begin{smallmatrix} \cos \theta & \sin \theta \\ -\sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{smallmatrix}\right) $$ Each of the $2 \times 2$ representations act on the corresponding subspace of the basis function. This creates the block diagonal form of the representation matrix. The representation $\rho(\mathbf{R}(\theta)^{\pm 1})$ is thus matrix-valued.
